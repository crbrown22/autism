AUTISM IN THE TRADITIONAL CLASSROOM

Challenges Arising from Insufficient Teacher Training, Resources, & Support to Accommodate the Diverse Needs of Austistic Children Within Traditional Classroms

The prevalence of ASD has surged in recent decades, with current estimates indicating that approximately one in 54 children in the United States is diagnosed with ASD (CDC, 2020). This escalating trend underscores the urgent need for educational systems to adapt and equip themselves with the requisite tools to accommodate the unique learning profiles of autistic children. Within traditional classrooms, educators encounter multifaceted challenges in adequately addressing the diverse needs of autistic children.

Recent studies emphasize the importance of integrating children diagnosed with autism spectrum disorder (ASD) into regular classroom environments. This integration not only fosters their social development, peer inclusion, and social awareness but also correlates directly with their developmental progress. The inclusion of children with autism in regular schools and their co-education with formally developing children in the same class is feasible and can lead to positive and encouraging results. (Alexandri, Papailiou, Nikolaou, 2017) Yannie et al. (2008) assert that regardless of the intervention setting, the number of weekly hours spent in the regular classroom significantly influences the developmental trajectory of ASD children. Literature comparing various intervention programs suggests that educational programs spanning 25 to 30 hours per week yield measurable progress (Yanni et al., 2008).

Misdiagnosis further complicates the situation, often resulting in challenging transitions for both the children and teachers. This misclassification can lead to autistic children being inappropriately grouped as emotionally disturbed rather than developmentally disabled (Bristol, 1980). Consequently, the proper placement of autistic children is frequently mistaken in classrooms designated for emotionally disturbed children. This research aims to address three critical questions:

  • How can administrators and school officials provide resources, training, personnel, and support to teachers to establish an all-inclusive classroom environment for ASD students?
  • How do teachers, psychologists, and administrators distinguish symptoms of ASD from behavioral issues?
  • What teaching techniques and philosophies will help bridge the gap between ASD students and regular students in the classroom?

The significance of this paper lies in advocating for inclusivity for ASD children within the educational spectrum, alongside the importance of accurate placement and differentiation between ASD children and those with behavioral issues. This paper delves into the intricate challenges arising from insufficient teacher training, resources, support, and misdiagnosis, all of which hinder the effective accommodation of autistic students in mainstream educational settings.

Despite the increasing prevalence of autism spectrum disorder (ASD) and the recognized benefits of integrating ASD students into regular classroom environments, educators face significant challenges in providing adequate support and accommodation due to insufficient training and resources. The current educational landscape lacks comprehensive strategies to equip teachers with the necessary skills, knowledge, and resources to create inclusive classroom environments for ASD students. This gap in training and resource allocation not only hampers the social, emotional, and academic development of ASD students but also exacerbates misdiagnosis and inappropriate placement within educational settings. Therefore, there is an urgent need to address the inadequacies in teacher training and resource provision to ensure that educators are equipped to effectively accommodate the diverse needs of ASD students within mainstream classrooms.

The integration of ASD students into regular classroom environments has become a significant area of study in education. Professionals argue that such integration fosters social, emotional, and academic development of these children but also benefits their typical peers. However, integration of ASD children into the regular classrooms does have its challenges. Bristol (1980) highlights the inadequacy of teacher training and resources, which can lead to a lack of preparedness in managing the unique characteristics and needs of ASD students. Adversely, the distinction between appropriate placement of autistic children and those with emotional disturbances is not always clear, which can lead to misclassifications and inappropriate grouping within classroom settings.

In light of these challenges, there is a pressing need for collaborative efforts among teachers and staff to develop effective strategies that promote inclusivity within traditional classroom settings. Comprehensive training programs and adequate resource allocation are essential to support students diagnosed with ASD effectively. Schools must prioritize the ability to discern between ASD and behavior disorders, as their approaches to intervention fundamentally differ.

Principal Proposition

Integrating children on the autism spectrum into regular classroom environments fosters their social, emotional, and academic development, with the duration and intensity of intervention programs playing crucial roles in their progress. Supportive environments are grounded in developmentally appropriate practices, where children feel accepted, cared for, and supported not only in their learning but also in their physical, emotional, and social well-being (Bredekamp and Copple, 1997). ASD students can acquire the social skills necessary for regular peer interaction, thereby enhancing their overall academic success. The sustainability of an integrated environment and its duration for each student depends on their position within the spectrum. Yannie et al. (2008) assert that regardless of the intervention setting, the number of weekly hours attended significantly influences the developmental progress of children with autism. Studies comparing different intervention programs indicate measurable educational advancements with programs spanning 25 to 30 hours per week (Yannie et al., 2008).

A primary rationale for placing spectrum students in mainstream classes is to enhance their opportunities for interaction with typical peers (Dahle, 2003). Bredekamp and Copple (1997) emphasize the importance of supportive environments grounded in developmentally appropriate practices, where children with autism spectrum disorder (ASD) feel accepted, cared for, and supported across various domains. ASD students can acquire essential social skills through regular peer interaction, which increases their overall academic success. Importantly, engagement of children with autism in classroom activities should be feasible, as well as the completion of the tasks assigned to them and the achievement of communication with teachers and their classmates. (Alexandri, Papailiou, Nikolaou, 2017) It is also important that the curriculum and the proposed activities be adapted and integrated into the needs and abilities of children with autism (Zoniu-Sideris, 2004; Taffa, 1998).

By encompassing these essential elements, educators can develop effective intervention methods and refine their teaching philosophies to create a comfortable and supportive learning environment for ASD students, traditional students, and teachers alike. Studies conducted by (Kourkoutas, 2011) and (Taffa, 1998) conclude the basic principle of inclusion is therefore to remove all kinds of prejudices and to try to ensure that all children have the same learning opportunities in environments that enable them to acquire new knowledge and develop their cognitive and socio-emotional abilities, to the extent that is feasible. The implementation of evidence-based strategies, alongside ongoing professional development opportunities, will empower educators to effectively address the diverse needs of ASD students. This approach will not only foster their social, emotional, and academic growth but also contribute to the overall positive atmosphere of the classroom.

Interacting Proposition

Nevertheless, inadequate training among teachers, staff, and students in managing situations related to the unique characteristics of students with ASD, coupled with limited resources for support, presents significant challenges. The integration, processing of outside information, development, prolonging of social relationships, and natural participation in unfamiliar environments often pose a challenge for individuals with ASD (Hoffman, 2009). In the Inclusion Notebook (2007) Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) is defined as deficits in social interactions, deficits in communication, restricted repertoire of interests and behaviors. (Inclusion Notebook, 2007, p. 2) Students identified with ASD generally demonstrate these behaviors or characteristics and, therefore, may hinder their efforts in the general education classroom. (Kwofie, 2008) Bristol (1980) also writes about the lack of teacher training and inadequate salaries which lead to lack of interest or being able to properly prepare themselves for the ASD student in the classroom. Humphrey & Symes' (2013) research indicates a shift in secondary school teachers' perceptions towards more positive views regarding the inclusion of children with autism. However, it also highlights the need for additional training to address teachers' perceived lack of knowledge and skills in teaching children with autism. (Alexandri, Papailiou, Nikolaou, 2017)

Additionally, the distinction between appropriate placement of autistic children and those with emotional disturbances is not always clear in classroom settings. Through this placement autistic children have been inappropriately grouped as emotionally disturbed rather than as developmentally disabled. The New England Journal of Medicine (2013) reported that in the 1950’s and 1960’s autism was recognized as a form of schizophrenia that affected children. (Bristol, 1980) Implementation of ASD children in the regular classroom can be difficult because they can suffer from a long list of distracting characteristics for a classroom environment which can range from tantrums, meltdowns, aggression, self-injury and destructive behavior. Engagement in repetitive, stereotyped behaviors or movements, restricted interests or obsessions, resistance to change, and abnormal responses to sensory stimulation are common characteristics of individuals with autism. (Kwofie, 2018) These distractions can lead to lack of production for the other students and overstimulation for ASD students. In addition to the distracting behavior, the misunderstanding of an ASD student's mental capacity can trigger overstimulation, anxiety, and isolation for these students. Bristol (1980) states there is a lack of school support for these students because they have been given the same curriculum by undertrained teachers, who lack the resources to help these students.

All of these factors can lead to unproductive and negative experiences for the classroom environment. The presence of distracting behaviors such as tantrums, meltdowns, and aggression can disrupt the learning environment for both ASD students and their peers. Furthermore, inadequate school support, coupled with undertrained teachers and a lack of tailored curriculum, exacerbates these challenges, resulting in unproductive and negative experiences for ASD students in the classroom.

Speculative Proposition

Hence, it is imperative for teachers and staff to collaborate in seeking effective strategies that promote inclusivity within traditional classroom settings, benefiting all students. A study by Nikolaou & Markogiannakis (2017) suggests that teachers' efforts are crucial for children's adaptation to the school environment and the promotion of their mental health. This indicates the importance of teacher support in facilitating the inclusion process for children with ASD.(Alexandri, Papailiou, Nikolaou, 2017) Through empirical analysis, the study identifies the provision of structure in place, time, and activities as the most influential teaching strategy predicting short and long-term academic growth in children with ASD. (Manti, Scholte, Van Berckelaer-Onnes, 2012) This necessitates the implementation of comprehensive training programs and allocation of resources to support students diagnosed with ASD.

The most important aspect of teaching a student with autism is to provide the students with opportunities to achieve, by breaking down tasks and immediately reinforcing the students for appropriate behavior. (Alexandri, Papailiou, Nikolaou, 2017) Schools must prioritize the ability to discern between ASD and behavior disorders, as although they may exhibit similar traits, the approaches to addressing these conditions fundamentally differ. Implementing systematic screening protocols, informed decision-making processes, and continuous evaluations will result in more accurate diagnoses and the formulation of appropriate intervention strategies. By employing such strategies, it becomes possible to differentiate between ASD-related symptoms and behavioral challenges with precision.

Drawing upon Walker and Avant’s 8-step concept method, fostering interactions and learning among peers within the same academic environment are strategies that facilitate the academic, social, and emotional development of ASD students. (Wu, Wang, 2002) Wu and Wang (2022) present the idea of peer mentoring support programs for students with autism spectrum disorder (ASD) in educational settings, emphasizing its potential to enhance their classroom success and social inclusiveness.

In addition to peer mentorship programs, training like the TEACCH summer seminar Bristol (1980) described as a two week convention with lectures and readings over the 9 topics can help teachers create a balanced environment while providing different teaching philosophies to benefit both the traditional and ASD students. TEACCH focuses on intervention for task development. This method “supports task completion by providing explicit instruction and visual support” specifically designed for each individual child in their own environment (Finch et al., 2013). Visual cues and recognition are considered great strategies for teaching ASD students because it can allow others to explain complex tasks and help them anticipate the future. TEACCH increases “imitation, perception, gross motor skills, hand-eye coordination, and cognitive performance” (Ryan et al., 2011, p. 59).

Along with teaching strategies, specific teaching elements have also been identified to aid teachers in educating children with peer mentoring support programs and training methodologies such as the TEACCH summer seminar, which emphasizes a balanced environment and diverse teaching philosophies, can benefit both ASD students and their typical peers (Wu & Wang, 2022; Bristol, 1980). Implementation of these strategies can facilitate interactions and learning among peers within the same academic environment, thereby promoting the academic, social, and emotional development of ASD students while enhancing their classroom success and social inclusiveness. Teachers must spend time getting to know their ASD students on a personal level, understanding their interests, strengths and weaknesses in order to help incorporate individualized goals and objectives in planning for classroom instruction. If there is a special class teacher, the process of fully including the child with autism would be beneficial for both the child himself and the class as a whole. (Kwofie, 2018)

By fostering flexibility, individualization, and collaboration, educators can cultivate a dynamic learning environment that caters to diverse learning styles. The 2012 (Manti, Scholte, Van Berckelaer-Onnes, 2012) research also highlights the importance of emotional support from teachers, peers and staff in order to encompass long-term academic gains. Children with autism who are educated in fully inclusive classrooms may require both social interaction interventions and behavioral interventions in order to effectively learn and relate to their classmates (Mazurik-Charles & Stefanou, 2010). This approach fosters meaningful participation and engagement for all students, promoting inclusivity and academic success.

The purpose of this study was to understand and identify the difficulties faced by teachers when including students with autism spectrum disorder (ASD) in the regular classroom due to lack of training and resources. Secondly the goal was to uncover best practices, strategies, solutions, and how to bridge the gap between teachers and students. This study involved a semi-structured interview collecting qualitative data on how teachers are hindered and what resources school can offer to better serve students with ASD. The semi-structured nature of the interviews allowed for flexibility in exploring participants' experiences and perspectives while ensuring that key topics related to the research objectives were addressed.

Research Context

Participants for the interviews were selected through targeting teachers with varying levels of experience in inclusive education and working with students with ASD across different educational settings. These measures were taken to ensure diversity in terms of demographics and teaching experience to capture a wide range of perspectives.

Participant Selection

A total of 4 semi-structured interviews were conducted with participating teachers. Each interview session lasted approximately 30 minutes and was audio-recorded with the consent of the participants. Teachers selected ranged from traditional classroom teachers to special education teachers from 2 different school districts. School district 1 has a total of about 5800 students whereas school district 2 has a total enrollment of around 500 students. Prior to conducting, informed consent was obtained from all participants, emphasizing voluntary participation, confidentiality, and the right to withdraw from the study at any time without repercussions.

Data Collection

During the interviews, participants were asked 11 open-ended questions designed to examine detailed accounts of their experiences, challenges faced, successful practices, and suggestions for improving support for students with ASD in regular classrooms. I had the opportunity to ask probing questions to delve deeper into specific topics and ensure comprehensive data collection. Having the opportunity to speak with these participants in an interview format, allowed me to collect additional information through conventional conversation in addition to the interview questions. By sitting down and collecting data through transcriptions, coding and categorization, I was able to gain sufficient enough information to analyze the data collected.

Data Analysis

Data analysis for this study followed a thematic approach, which involved several stages aimed at uncovering meaningful patterns and themes within the interview transcripts. Initially, the transcripts were carefully reviewed and systematically coded to identify relevant segments of text related to the research objectives. During the coding process, key concepts, ideas, and phrases were assigned descriptive labels or codes to capture the essence of the content. This process allowed for the organization and categorization of data into manageable units for further analysis. Once the initial coding was complete, the coded segments were grouped together based on shared characteristics or themes. This process of categorization involved comparing and contrasting coded segments to identify similarities and differences, thus facilitating the identification of recurring themes emerging from the data.

Throughout the analysis, careful attention was paid to the context and nuances of the data, allowing for a nuanced understanding of the issues and perspectives expressed by the participants. This approach enabled me to delve deeper into the content of the conversations and extract rich insights relevant to the research objectives. To aid in the analysis process, charts, matrices, or other visual representations may have been utilized to organize and visualize the data, facilitating the identification of patterns and trends across the dataset. These visual aids helped to synthesize the findings and identify key areas of focus for further exploration. By systematically analyzing the transcripts and identifying recurring patterns and themes, the researcher was able to gain a comprehensive understanding of the issues and challenges faced by teachers in inclusive education settings. This methodological approach facilitated the extraction of valuable insights, which informed the subsequent discussion and implications of the study's findings.

In this study, a qualitative research approach was employed to explore the experiences and perspectives of educators regarding the integration of children on the autism spectrum into regular classroom environments. Specifically, a case study design was utilized to delve deeply into the context of individual classrooms and the nuanced strategies employed by educators to support ASD students. The case study approach allowed for an in-depth examination of the integration process, focusing on the unique challenges, strategies, and outcomes observed within each classroom setting. The data analysis process involved multiple stages, beginning with the transcription of interviews conducted with educators who had experience integrating ASD students into mainstream classrooms. These transcripts were then subjected to a thorough coding process, where codes were generated to capture key concepts, ideas, and patterns within the data. These initial codes were then organized into overarching themes, guided by the research questions and the emergent patterns observed in the data.

Theme 1: Holistic Development and Inclusion

This theme encompasses the perceived benefits of integrating ASD students into regular classroom environments, focusing on their social, emotional, and academic development, as well as the sense of inclusion fostered within the classroom community. Educators emphasized the importance of peer relationships and the sense of belonging experienced by ASD students. For example, one educator noted, "Integrating children with ASD into regular classrooms provides invaluable opportunities for their holistic development, including social, emotional, and academic growth."

Theme 2: Supportive Environment Strategies

This theme highlights the strategies employed by educators to create a supportive and inclusive environment for ASD students within mainstream classrooms. Examples of such strategies include teacher training on ASD awareness, individualized accommodations, and promoting peer acceptance through inclusive activities. One educator stated, "Effective strategies include teacher training on ASD awareness, implementing individualized accommodations, and promoting peer acceptance through inclusive activities."

Theme 3: Challenges

The challenges theme explores the various obstacles faced by educators when integrating ASD students into mainstream classrooms. These challenges include managing behavioral difficulties, addressing sensory sensitivities, and navigating limited resources. Educators emphasized the importance of implementing nuanced strategies to address these challenges effectively. For instance, one educator noted, "Common challenges include managing behavioral difficulties, addressing sensory sensitivities, and providing individualized support with limited resources."

Theme 4: Critical Support and Resources

This theme focuses on the critical support and resources needed to effectively meet the needs of ASD students within regular classroom environments. Examples include ongoing professional development, collaboration with specialists, and individualized education plans tailored to the unique needs of each student. Educators emphasized the importance of access to evidence-based practices and assistive technology. As one educator stated, "Access to evidence-based practices and assistive technology is crucial for equipping educators to effectively support ASD students in mainstream classrooms."

Theme 5: Classification and Placement

Kerri stresses the importance of comprehensive assessments, input from parents and specialists, and collaboration with mental health professionals in distinguishing appropriate placements for ASD students. Mark emphasizes the utilization of evidence-based tools and collaboration with mental health professionals to ensure accurate classification and placement aligned with each student's needs. These quotes underscore the significance of thorough assessment and collaborative decision-making in determining the most suitable educational placements for ASD students.

Conclusion

The prevalence of autism spectrum disorder (ASD) has underscored the imperative for educational systems to adapt and equip themselves to accommodate the unique learning profiles of autistic children. This research has focused on exploring the challenges arising from insufficient teacher training, resources, and support in traditional classrooms, particularly within the context of Oklahoma's educational landscape. The research topic delved into the urgent need for comprehensive strategies to equip educators with the necessary skills and resources to create inclusive learning environments for ASD students. Through an examination of existing literature and qualitative interviews with educators, several key points have emerged, which are vital for informing educational policy and practice.

One of the central findings of this research is the significant gap in teacher training and resource allocation, hindering the effective accommodation of ASD students. Misdiagnosis and inappropriate placement are prevalent, exacerbating the challenges faced by both students and educators. Collaborative efforts among educators, administrators, and support staff are essential to address these challenges and develop effective strategies for promoting inclusivity within traditional classroom settings. Comprehensive training programs and ongoing professional development opportunities are crucial in supporting ASD students effectively. By equipping educators with the necessary skills and knowledge, educational institutions can ensure that ASD students receive the support they need to thrive academically, socially, and emotionally.

The significance of these findings extends beyond the confines of this research paper. They have profound implications for Oklahoma's education system and its commitment to providing equitable educational opportunities for all students. By addressing the identified challenges and implementing evidence-based practices, Oklahoma can lead the way in creating inclusive educational environments that benefit all students, including those with autism spectrum disorder. In conclusion, this research underscores the critical need for action to improve the educational experiences of ASD students in Oklahoma. By fostering collaboration, providing comprehensive training, and allocating resources effectively, Oklahoma can position itself as a leader in inclusive education, ensuring that every student has the opportunity to reach their full potential.
